SUMMARY

Concept Sketch Development is designed to allow students to solve creative problems each week, but not necessarily take every idea to its final product. This course teaches quick and agile thinking.

DESCRIPTION

The objective of this course is to focus on concept development and creative problem solving, presenting ideas in sketch form rather than fully realized pieces/products. Each week new ideas will be presented to the group as if a client were looking for a designer to take on a particular project.

COURSE METHODS

Our work in this course will be accomplished through: demonstrations, in-class studio work, lectures, image and video presentations, one-on-one instruction, group discussions/critiques, online tutorials, research and out-of-class homework. Participation is crucial to achieve the learning objectives.

Please ask me for ideas if you want to add complexity to a project. If there is insufficient time in class, please arrange to work with me one-on-one during office hours.

There is no textbook required for this course. For the purpose of discussion and for research, students will be required to read materials and watch tutorials given in class, via books provided in class, readings stored in the Blueline classroom, and via websites. We will have a test at Mid-term and a final project. I will routinely give online links and bring books and magazines to class for you to either read or browse for specific project research or general research on graphics, design and/or illustration trends. Written responses will be required to some readings, images, design and videos.

BASICS OF CLASS PROCEDURES / A FEW RULES

After the day’s presentation/lecture and/or demonstration - you must work only on class projects during in-class work-time. If you finish a project early, add complexity. If you prefer to work on your own laptop, that is fine – bring it to class. After studio time has begun, quickly ready your materials and begin working as soon as possible. You may talk quietly to classmates during work time, but talking must not impede production. You must work hard the whole class period and you will be expected to work out of class also – our class meets for four hours a week so expect to work two to four hours outside of class. If you have a creative block, or if you don’t know what you want to produce, please talk to me.

NO CELL PHONES are to be used during class unless prior arrangements are made and if you receive an emergency call – please take it outside the classroom.

NO TEXT MESSAGING, INSTANT MESSAGING, CHECKING EMAIL OR SOCIAL MEDIA will be allowed during studio time.

Personal stereos (i.e. a MP3 players) and/or headphones are allowed in class ONLY while independently working and after I have given permission but you must be able to still hear me speak and when I address the class, pause the music and take off your headphones.

No food and drink are allowed in the labs. If caught, you will be expected to leave class and dispose of the items.

Students are expected to participate in class. Inappropriate behavior such as reading non-course materials, checking email, instant or text messag- ing, studying for other classes or sleeping will result in dismissal from that day’s class and count as an unexcused absence.

Students are accountable for turning in all work on time. Faculty will either penalize late assignments or refuse to accept them.

Working in groups is part of all communications work. Students who fail to do their share of work on team projects or are unwilling to participate in group problem solving will be dismissed from the teams and lose credit for projects.

Students are always welcome to seek more information about why they received a particular grade on a test or assignment, obtain guidance for how to improve work and ask for suggestions on solving problems. They are encouraged to go above and beyond the minimum work assigned and to demonstrate enthusiasm for work in their fields. In the JMC Department C work is minimal work. A and B work goes beyond the minimum.

Critiques / Feedback

Except for the final project, we will critique your sketches directly from your sketchbooks. However, when you turn in your finals for the grade, you will need to scan them and create a multi-page pdf.

You will participate in critiques a couple times in the semester. At times these will occur with the whole class providing feedback and at other times the class will be broken into smaller groups or you will be assigned a few classmates names and you will write responses, suggestions, etc and leave the feedback in text form in their digital box on the instructors computer or via blueline. The format and content of these critiques will change – varying from verbal to written responses, often answering specific instructor-provided questions. The small group responses will be followed up with the instructor or as a large group discussion. Critiques will get easier the more you do them. You must learn to accept feedback and also learn to defend your choices. Most critiques will consist of your professor giving feedback, sometimes the students will lead the critiques as the semester progresses on bigger projects.

GRADING CRITERIA

Points can be earned in different categories via a rubric – some categories can impact grades more than others. It is very difficult to achieve “A” quality work in the class. An “A” project will be considered professional level design, and perfect in the majority of grading categories. B+ and B grades will be given to good work that is well above course expectations.

Simply meeting the expectations of the course in completing assignments, quizzes, projects and the practical will result in a C grade. Doing the minimum amount of work to get by is not going to earn you a good grade. In order to earn grades higher than a C, I expect students to show sub- stantial growth in critical skills, mastery of the technical elements of the course, and perfect execution of design concepts in major projects. D and F work will fail to meet expectations.

Being late for a critique (including not having your assignment prepared when class begins) will result in a 10% “late fee” on that grade the moment that critique starts, and through the first 24 hours after critique starts. Another 10% will be assessed in the second 24 hour period. Late assignments cannot be revised.

Failure to turn in any assignment will result in a maximum course grade of D. I reserve the right to reject assignments after the second 24 hour period has expired, thus triggering the automatic maximum course grade of D.

GRADING CATEGORIES

Each project will be graded according to how well the student fulfilled the objectives of the project. However, participation and personal growth are important factors in the evaluation. All assignments are graded by the criteria of the 6 categories listed below but additional categories or factors may be added. In-class projects, research, critiques, homework projects, and final projects are assigned different values. The 6 categories of grading will help you identify your strengths and weaknesses. By observing recurring patterns or problem areas, you can change your outcome. Each project will value the different categories by different percentages.
